# The Ajax Line: hold the line on collective attorney brain-trust sovereignty

## White-paper thesis

Large law firms should treat their collective attorney brain trust as a
strategically important asset category and independently maintain a defensible
boundary around their own instances of it. The asset is not
only documents or client facts. It includes recurring interactions among
people, tasks, tools, judgment calls, exceptions, rationales, outcomes,
economics, and governance.

If multiple firms permit those interaction patterns to be retained,
aggregated, or used to improve an upstream frontier capability, they may create
an industry-level externality. Each contributing firm could strengthen a
system capable of reproducing overlapping portions of the professional
judgment on which all firms compete. A firm that protects its own knowledge can
still be exposed when competitors disclose substantially overlapping patterns.

This is the **Ajax Line**: each firm should independently protect collective
professional knowledge while still using AI behind firm-controlled governance.
Open, voluntary, nonexclusive technical or professional standards can make
those unilateral decisions easier to implement. The name is a memorable
Trojan War alias; the descriptive thesis remains authoritative.

## Epistemic status

This is a candidate normative thesis supported by confirmed facts, bounded
inferences, and unresolved hypotheses. It is not evidence that Anthropic or any
other named provider currently pools law-firm ontologies, trains on ordinary
commercial content, or intends to disintermediate law firms.

The strongest supportable wording is **potential existential competitive
threat to the present large-law differentiation and economic model**. It is not
yet supportable to call it a proven existential threat to law, legal
institutions, or any named firm.

## Why one firm's boundary may not be enough

### 1. The relevant asset overlaps across firms

Elite firms have proprietary combinations of talent, experience, relationships,
process, judgment, and culture. They also operate over many common legal
domains, counterparties, courts, transaction structures, regulatory regimes,
and professional methods. Their ontologies are not identical, but they overlap.

The working hypothesis is:

```text
many firms' partially overlapping judgment traces
  -> normalized issue, option, exception, and outcome graph
  -> broader frontier evaluation and strategy capability
  -> reduced scarcity of each firm's overlapping professional knowledge
  -> pressure on differentiation, pricing, training, and firm economics
```

The system does not need to recreate a firm perfectly to affect competition.
It may be enough to reproduce a valuable common denominator, identify which
exceptions matter, and search combinations faster than any single firm.

### 2. A holdout can face an externality created by peers

Suppose Firm A keeps its ontology, evaluation sets, attorney rationales, and
outcome loops inside a firm-controlled system. Firms B through Z expose similar
workflows and overlapping judgment patterns to an upstream system under terms
that permit retention, aggregation, evaluation, or improvement. Firm A may
still face a stronger system trained or refined on the shared portion of the
market's knowledge.

That does not mean Firm A's unique culture, relationships, confidential facts,
or rare judgment have been copied. It means the protective value of unilateral
secrecy can decline as the external system learns from substitutes.

Kirkland & Ellis is a motivating example in the owner's hypothesis, but the
current repository lacks complete primary evidence about Kirkland's actual
architecture and contractual controls. The public paper must therefore present
the Kirkland application as a research question, not a settled company claim.

### 3. Frontier advantage could include synthesis, search, and gaming

The risk is broader than memorizing a precedent or playbook. A frontier system
could potentially combine:

- pattern recognition across matters, practices, and firms;
- search over option, countermove, and outcome spaces;
- simulation of counterpart, judge, regulator, or client responses;
- synthesis of strategies no single source used;
- evaluation and iterative self-critique;
- transfer of common structures across adjacent legal problems.

These remain capability hypotheses for law. They require legal-domain
benchmarks and cannot be inferred from general model progress alone.

## The Move 37 analogy

The famous AlphaGo event was **Move 37 in Game 2** against Lee Sedol. Google
DeepMind's ten-year retrospective says professional commentators initially
thought the unconventional move was a mistake, but it proved decisive much
later. AlphaGo combined learned evaluation, search, reinforcement learning, and
human expert games.

The analogy is useful because it illustrates a discontinuity in strategic
search: a system can select a move outside prevailing professional convention
and reveal its value only after a long sequence.

The analogy has strict limits:

- Go is a closed, perfect-information game with explicit rules and a measurable
  win condition; law is open, institutional, normative, adversarial, and
  fact-dependent.
- Legal success includes duties, legitimacy, persuasion, relationships,
  uncertainty, client objectives, and consequences that resist a single reward.
- AlphaGo's result does not prove legal creativity, legal correctness, or
  professional judgment.

AlphaGo Zero is also a critical counterpoint. It reached superhuman Go
performance using self-play without human game data beyond the rules. Holding
the line may therefore preserve law-firm differentiation and deny an easy
shortcut, but it cannot guarantee that frontier systems will require private
law-firm knowledge to become formidable.

## Ten years of progress: bounded claim

Research supports saying that training compute devoted to important systems and
measured algorithmic efficiency on studied benchmarks advanced materially over
the decade following AlphaGo. It does not support assuming indefinite
exponential growth, a universal doubling law, or a predictable date for
superhuman legal strategy.

The NeurIPS study in `SRC-0045` estimates substantial reductions in compute
needed to reach fixed language-model benchmark thresholds over 2012-2023. Its
estimate is benchmark-, data-, model-, and period-specific. Future white-paper
language must preserve those limits.

## What “hold the line” means operationally

Holding the line does not mean refusing AI. It means using AI without silently
transferring the firm's control plane or granting upstream rights in derived
professional knowledge.

Minimum governance questions include:

1. Are ordinary inputs, outputs, feedback, evaluations, telemetry, embeddings,
   and derived representations excluded from generalized training and product
   improvement?
2. Does the restriction cover semantic derivatives, patterns, labels,
   rankings, and evaluation results—not only raw documents?
3. Can data or derived representations cross matters, clients, tenants, firms,
   model families, or provider product teams?
4. Are prompts, corrections, accept/reject decisions, redlines, tool sequences,
   escalation paths, and outcome loops minimized and retained only as needed?
5. Are the firm's ontology, evaluation sets, and policy layer firm-controlled,
   exportable, replaceable, and usable with more than one model?
6. Can the firm audit access, verify deletion, test memorization and
   re-identification, and leave without losing its operating memory?
7. Are optional feedback, support, co-development, and research programs
   separately authorized instead of bundled with ordinary service?

Any sector work must remain limited to open, voluntary, nonexclusive
professional-responsibility, security, privacy, interoperability, and
data-governance standards. Each firm must decide independently whether and how
to adopt a standard or contract with a provider. Competitors must not agree to
refuse a vendor, deal only on jointly imposed terms, pressure suppliers
collectively, exchange current procurement or negotiating positions, or
coordinate prices, clients, markets, hiring, or other competitively sensitive
conduct. Sector activity requires competition-law review.
`SRC-0049` records the FTC's current group-boycott guidance supporting the
independent-decision boundary. This project is not legal advice.

## Strongest counterarguments

1. **No evidence of current pooling.** Current public evidence does not
   establish covert ontology aggregation by Anthropic or another named lab.
2. **Commercial controls may work.** Contracts, zero-data-retention options,
   tenant isolation, and technical controls could prevent the hypothesized
   externality.
3. **AI may complement firms.** Better tools can increase lawyer productivity,
   quality, access, and firm differentiation rather than destroy it.
4. **Much law is public or common.** Statutes, opinions, public filings, forms,
   and recurring methods limit how proprietary the common layer can be.
5. **Tacit judgment may not transfer.** Knowledge embedded in relationships and
   organizational context may lose value when removed from the originating
   firm.
6. **The Go analogy may mislead.** Law lacks a stable board, complete state,
   fixed rules, clean reward, and cheap self-play.
7. **Frontier systems may not need the data.** AlphaGo Zero shows that a system
   can sometimes surpass human expertise without training on expert examples.

## Falsifiers and confirmation conditions

The thesis should weaken if independent evidence shows that:

- ordinary and optional provider paths technically prevent retention,
  generalized improvement, and cross-customer derivation, including semantic
  derivatives;
- law-firm ontology overlap is too low for peer exposure to create useful
  substitutes;
- models trained on multi-firm abstractions do not improve legal strategy,
  evaluation, or workflow performance over public-only baselines;
- firm-controlled architecture produces no measurable differentiation or exit
  advantage;
- legal judgment remains strongly non-transferable outside the originating
  team and matter context; or
- frontier legal capability plateaus despite additional compute, algorithms,
  public data, tools, and synthetic experience.

The thesis should strengthen only with evidence such as:

- contract or technical proof of provider rights in cross-customer derived
  representations;
- reproducible tests showing multi-firm interaction traces improve performance
  over public-only and single-firm baselines;
- evidence that operational and judgment graphs remain useful after content
  minimization;
- measured reductions in differentiation, switching power, or professional
  training capacity linked to upstream capability; or
- a validated legal-strategy benchmark exhibiting robust, unconventional,
  delayed-value decisions comparable in structure—not rhetoric—to Move 37.

## Dedicated research lane

The Cassandra Radar should monitor:

- provider terms covering derived data, evaluation, feedback, telemetry,
  embeddings, support, and product improvement;
- legal co-development, forward-deployed engineering, managed services,
  acquisitions, and alternative business structures;
- multi-customer learning, federated learning, privacy-preserving aggregation,
  and cross-tenant evaluation methods;
- legal-agent simulation, self-play, debate, search, planning, and game-theoretic
  evaluation;
- empirical measures of algorithmic efficiency and training/inference compute;
- portability, firm-owned ontologies, model-neutral evaluation, audit, deletion,
  and derived-rights clauses;
- effects on associate training, partner formation, pricing, differentiation,
  client choice, professional independence, and access to justice.

Every signal must state whether it is observed fact, inference, hypothesis, or
unknown; carry a source revision and locator; preserve counterevidence; and
remain candidate-only until independent and human review.
